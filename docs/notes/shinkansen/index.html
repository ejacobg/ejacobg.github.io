Train Types

Some services are faster/have less stops than others, similar to the distinction between the local/rapid services on the non-Shinkansen lines.

The Nishi-Kyushu Shinkansen between Nagasaki and Takeo-Onsen has only one service type, the Kamome.

The Kyushu Shinkansen between Hakata and Kagoshima-Chuo operates 3 services, in order of speed: Mizuho, Sakura, and Tsubame.

The Sanyo Shinkansen between Hakata and Kokura offers 5 services (Mizuho, Sakura, Nozomi, Hikari, and Kodama), but the distinction between them isn’t particularly relevant since Hakata and Kokura are adjacent to each other.

Tickets

Most services offer both reserved and non-reserved seating. When buying reserved tickets, do note that the reservation is for a particular train, date, and time. If you miss your train, then your reservation is no longer valid on later services. You may still be able to board other services, but only in the non-reserved cars. (Don’t quote me on this.)

Reserved seats are recommended for longer or busier routes. You can always buy your tickets the day of, and just make a reservation for the next train.

Reserved and non-reserved tickets are their own tickets, separate from the “base fare” ticket (paid at the train gates using a paper ticket or IC card). Shinkansen tickets must be inserted into the gate alongside your base fare. You may sometimes receive a single paper ticket that contains both your base and Shinkansen fare.

Note: I see some conflicting sources on whether mixed-payment (IC + paper ticket) options are allowed. Inserting 2 paper tickets should always be accepted though.
